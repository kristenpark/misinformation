Misinformation

The purpose of this website is to educate people about misinformation and how to protect yourself from it.

It was created by David Chang, Max Guo, Apollo Lee, and Kristen Park. It uses HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

Types of Misinformation

  • Fabricated content: the deliberate spread of false information.
    • An example is deep fakes, which uses deep learning to falsify images, videos, and audio in order to deceive people
  • Manipulation: distorting information or images, clickbait
  • Impersonating a well-known source, such as a news agency
  • Misleading content, such as comments presented as facts or misleading graphs
  • Satire and parody, when false stories are made to seem real to make fun of something, such as in The Onion. These may accidentally fool readers into thinking the information presented is true.
  • Unreliable sources, such as random people on social media or an unnamed source in an article
  • Bias, when the information presented is in favor or against a certain idea or thing

Misinformation dominates discourse on platforms such as Twitter
  • According to a study led by MIT, false information dominated the discourse on social media platforms such as Twitter for the past 10 years.
  • According to the study, it is much easier for fake news to spread
    • It takes about 6 times less time for fake news to transmit to 1500 people than it does for real, credible information
  • Fake news is 70% more likely to be spread
  • Roughly around 40% of all Americans obtain their news through social media
How can we fix this?
  • Redesigning social media to better promote credibility is quite difficult
  • As it stands, restricting information on social media platforms seems like the most feasible solution. However, while many platforms are trying to do this, misinformation still spreads quicker than it is sealed up.

Misinformation and Trust

  • As of September, 2019, 41% of Americans trust mass media
    • 13% of Americans claim to wholeheartedly trust the news
    • The other 28% claim that they trust the news “a good deal”
  • In 2016, around the election, trust in the news was around 32%, an all time low

Impact of Misinformation

  • Widespread, false ideas using the internet as a medium
    • Cloud the public’s general understanding of important issues, which has great influence on beliefs
    • Leads to incorrect and sometimes harmful choices
  • Creates social tension by manipulating emotions
    • Can cause unjustified fear/suspicions in addition to anger
    • People may take action, such as protesting
  • Continually leads to more misinformation
    • People will confidently state their beliefs, which are based of originally incorrect assumptions
    • Like spreading a rumor
  • Affect many levels of society
    • Examples: Political elections (speeches, propaganda) to consumer choices (advertisements)

How To Protect Yourself

  1. Take note of the source of the information and if it’s credible. The source should be named, or else concealed for the safety of the source.
  2. Don’t read only the headline.
  3. Cross-reference and use multiple sources to see if they contain the same information. Some news articles are designed to encourage emotion from the reader instead of inform.
  4. Pay attention to possible biases in the sources of information.
  5. Read graphs carefully and pay attention to the wording of the text. For example, something may only be a scientific hypothesis, not a fact.

COVID-19 Misinformation

  • “COVID-19 Misinformation Pandemic”
    • Given this nickname due to the staggering amount of false information associated with the disease
  • Misinformation likely spread to induce fear and panic in the general public
  • Sites like Poynter work to dispel false claims made about the disease
